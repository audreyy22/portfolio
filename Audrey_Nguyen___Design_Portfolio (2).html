Triton-To-Go Redesign
App Redesign · UX Research
Usability Testing User Research User Interviews
The UCSD Triton-To-Go mobile ordering app is widely used by students to order meals and groceries from campus restaurants and markets. Despite its popularity, the app contained several friction points that frustrated users and caused ordering mistakes. Our team conducted user research with 15 participants to identify and redesign the 4 most critical usability issues.
UX Researcher · Designer (team of 5)
Nov – Dec 2024
Designed and ran structured usability tests with 15 UCSD students. Pre-task questions established baseline goals and app familiarity. Two observation tasks tested users locating specific restaurants and using the Just Walk Out feature. Post-task interviews used the Master–Apprentice model to surface honest pain points and comparisons with apps like UberEats and DoorDash. We identified four recurring error patterns across participants.

Design Walkthrough — 4 Problems & Redesigns

Problem 1 — Feature Discoverability

6/15 users struggled to find the Just Walk Out feature, which was buried in a list alongside rarely-used functions despite being one of the most-used. Users didn't know it existed, leading to avoidable friction.

Redesign

Moved Just Walk Out to the bottom navigation bar for immediate discoverability, reducing action-based slips.

Redesigned navigation — Just Walk Out & filter added

Problem 2 — Vague Food Descriptions

7/15 users said photos and reviews would help them decide. Vague text-only descriptions led to knowledge-based mistakes where users ordered dishes they didn't expect.

Redesign

Added dish photos and star ratings to food detail pages, giving users the visual context needed to make informed decisions.

Redesigned food detail page with photo & star ratings

Problem 3 — Poor Restaurant Filtering

9/15 users had trouble finding restaurants not near them. The app automatically sorted by proximity with no manual override, leaving students unable to browse the full selection on their own terms.

Redesign

Introduced a filter menu (alphabetical, distance, wait time) so users can sort restaurants by their own preferences.

Filter menu redesign — sort by distance, wait time, or alphabetical

Problem 4 — Mistimed Closure Feedback

9/15 users encountered a rule-based mistake where the app let them select items and reach checkout before revealing the restaurant had stopped taking orders. This wasted time and caused frustration.

Redesign

Added an immediate pop-up alert when a restaurant stops accepting orders, preventing users from reaching checkout only to be turned away.

Immediate closure alert — notifies users before they begin ordering

Each redesign was mapped to a design space trade-off. Transparency vs. Simplicity for the food detail changes, and Personalization vs. Simplicity for the filtering feature, positioning our redesign closer to the ideal than both the original app and competitors like UberEats. This project taught me how much small friction points compound across a user's experience, and how rigorous observation methodology can surface issues that surveys alone would miss.

Global Gender Equality Heatmap
Data Visualization · Python · MPI
Data Viz Python pygame MPI pandas

Final heatmap — 2023. Each row is a country, each column a type of gender equality legislation. Warm/bright = long-adopted law; cool/dark = recent or absent.

An animated data visualization tracking the global adoption of five categories of gender equality legislation, equal employment, night/dangerous work rights, anti-discrimination, sexual harassment, and domestic violence laws, across 195 countries from 1970 to 2023. Each frame of the animation is a heatmap where rows represent countries and columns represent law types, with color and brightness encoding how long a country has held each law. As the animation progresses through the years, the visualization brightens and warms, making the spread of legislation visible as a living pattern rather than a static chart.
Designer · Developer (solo)
Oct – Dec 2025
Built in Python using Pygame for graphics and window management, MPI for parallel processing across four ranks (each handling a display quadrant), and NumPy and pandas for data processing. Multiple CSV datasets were merged and processed to extract the earliest year each country adopted each law. That value was normalized to 0–1 and mapped to HSV color: hue encodes law duration (warm = long-adopted, cool = recent/absent) and brightness mirrors duration as well, so the most established legislation appears both warmer and brighter. Heatmap surfaces are generated per year with blurred fade transitions between frames.
The project went through three distinct stages before reaching the final form.

Iteration 1 — grayscale

Generated static heatmap PNGs from the raw data in grayscale to validate the data structure and layout. Each row and column mapped correctly but offered no color differentiation between law types or durations. Used as proof of concept before adding visual encoding.

Iteration 2 — limited color

Moved to live heatmap generation on the cluster with animated transitions between years. Introduced a limited color palette to test the HSV mapping, but contrast was low. The structure worked; the visual encoding needed refinement.

Iteration 3 — final: full HSV encoding + brightness

Expanded the color palette to full HSV range and added brightness as a secondary data dimension so both hue and brightness now reflect law duration, creating a richer and more readable result. Added blur-fade transitions between years and resized to fit display dimensions. The final animation runs 1970–2023 continuously, growing visibly warmer and brighter as global legislation expands.

The completed visualization runs as an animated loop on display clusters, cycling through each year from 1970 to 2023. Watch the full animation below.
This was my first time building a data visualization intended to run on physical display hardware, and the constraints of the cluster environment pushed me to think carefully about performance and rendering. The most interesting design challenge was finding a color encoding that felt intuitive. Tying both hue and brightness to the same data dimension came from noticing that either alone wasn't legible enough. Watching the visualization brighten and warm over 50 years gives the data an emotional quality that a static chart wouldn't. I started this project wanting to experiment with color and ended up having a much deeper understanding of how color communicates data.

Invert Industries
Label Design & Brand Systems · Marketing Internship
Print Design adobe suite Brand Systems Iterative Design
Click to upload a cover image or screenshot
During my marketing internship at Invert Industries, I worked on two interconnected design challenges: redesigning a product container label that was incompatible with manufacturing constraints, and building brand standards to address a lack of visual consistency across the company's content. The label work involved multiple rounds of iteration alongside real-world production testing.
Marketing Intern · Label Designer
Jan – Jun 2025
The original label had an ingredients list printed sideways, making it hard to read. My supervisors asked me to make it vertical. There were two versions of the label, one with a solid background and one with a botanical print. The solid background was straightforward to edit in InDesign. The printed version was significantly harder as I couldn't simply replace the text without disrupting the underlying illustration. I had to identify and match the exact font used on the label, recreate the text from scratch, and carefully realign it to be consistent with the rest of the layout. This was my first time working within an existing design rather than building from zero, which demanded much more attention to alignment, type formatting, and visual consistency with the parts I hadn't touched.

Label 2 — version 1 (solid background)

Label 2 — version 2 (botanical print)

The revised label was used in a live test of the shrink label application process. The test revealed an unexpected manufacturing constraint which was that anything not on the leading edge of the label became distorted when passing through the heat tunnel. The canvas size was also significantly smaller than anticipated. I had to inspect the failed labels to understand exactly where distortion occurred, then rework the layout so all critical information sat within the safe leading edge zone. This meant deciding what could be removed and what was legally or functionally necessary and balancing content hierarchy with production reality. It was a lesson in designing for manufacturing, not just aesthetics.
Alongside the label project, I identified a lack of consistency in Invert's visual content across channels. I compiled a set of brand assets and established standards covering color, typography, and layout to ensure cohesion in future materials. This gave me experience thinking systemically about a brand rather than solving one-off design problems.
This internship was formative in understanding real-world design constraints. Working within an existing design system, matching someone else's typographic choices, and redesigning after a production failure all required a level of precision and adaptability I hadn't fully encountered in class projects. I learned not every project works on the first try and that revising after setbacks is an important part of the process.
